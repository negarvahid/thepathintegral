The **Variational Quantum Eigensolver (VQE)** is one of the most important algorithms
in near-term quantum computing. The goal is simple: find the lowest energy (ground state)
of a quantum system. The method is clever: use a classical optimizer to tune the
parameters of a quantum circuit until the measured energy stops going down.

## The core idea

Given a Hamiltonian $H$ (an operator encoding the physics of your system), we want:

$$
E_0 = \min_\theta \langle \psi(\theta) | H | \psi(\theta) \rangle
$$

The quantum circuit $|\psi(\theta)\rangle$ is called the **ansatz** — a parameterized
guess at the ground state. The classical optimizer tunes $\theta$ to minimize the
expectation value.

## What you're watching

**Circuit panel** — The parameterized ansatz circuit. Each $R_y$ gate has a tunable
angle $\theta_i$. After each optimizer step the angles change.

**Bloch spheres** — Each qubit's quantum state visualized on a sphere.
The north pole is $|0\rangle$, south pole is $|1\rangle$, equator is superposition.

**Energy plot** — The expectation value $\langle H \rangle$ at each iteration.
The green dashed line is the exact ground truth (from full diagonalization).

**State distribution** — Probability of measuring each basis state. At convergence
this should concentrate on the ground state.

## Why it works

The variational principle guarantees that for any state $|\psi\rangle$:

$$
\langle \psi | H | \psi \rangle \geq E_0
$$

So the expectation value is always an **upper bound** on the true ground state energy.
A classical optimizer (COBYLA, Powell, Nelder-Mead) minimizes this upper bound —
and if the ansatz is expressive enough, it converges to $E_0$.

## Limitations

VQE is **heuristic** — there's no guarantee the optimizer finds the global minimum,
and the quality of the result depends heavily on the choice of ansatz. For molecules
like H₂ the UCCSD-inspired ansatz is theoretically motivated; for general problems
you're trading expressibility against trainability.

The bigger challenge at scale is the **barren plateau** problem: for deep circuits
with many qubits, gradients vanish exponentially and training stalls.
